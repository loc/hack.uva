FROM THE EXPERTS

Design Thinking with Professor Dana Elzey


On Grounds, Professor Elzey teaches several classes related to design thinking. Given the nature of hackathons, we thought it’d be beneficial to hear his insight into effective problem-solving. Hopefully, this short Q&A will help you become a better hacker!

What should a team consider first when approaching a problem?

“A certain level of trust is a basis, a foundation, for effective collaboration.”

If you haven’t worked together before, Professor Elzey recommends establishing trust between team members. It’s beneficial to socialize with your peers outside of the work environment. Get to know each other! Share stories! Building these personal connections can lead to more productive teamwork and better overall projects.

When developing a solution, are there any areas that you’d think an engineering or computer science student might overlook?

“Nothing is worse than a good idea, if you only have one of them.”

Professor Elzey warns against prematurely stopping the brainstorming process once a good idea has been found. Instead, he encourages students to generate several potential ideas before focusing on one solution. He calls this the value of “getting unstuck”. A team should exhaustively search the solution-space until they get stuck. Then, after a mental break, they keep looking until they “get unstuck”. The most promising solutions can often arise in the process of overcoming these mental blocks.

Since Hackathons typically only last 24 hours, how can a team quickly determine the root issue?

His main piece of advice: be graphical. Looking at visualizations of the data can elucidate things about the problem and potential solutions. Techniques such as a why-why or Duncker diagram can help you determine the underlying issue and provide infrastructure for brainstorming.
